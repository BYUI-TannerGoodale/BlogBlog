Sourcing the Product

What do you have to offer and how can you monetize it? I suppose this is the key question to ask when your looking to open a business. This week I had a chance to study up on different business models and source product ideas. My class was introduced to Google Trends this week and we were encouraged to look for products to focus on as well as a business model to build on. We covered the classic merchant model, manufacturer model, drop shipping model and the affiliate model. I'll attempt to give a brief summery of those four.

Merchant Model

The merchant model is the classic ideal of a business. You purchase product at wholesale prices and resell them at a markup to turn a profit. There isn't much more to it. In order to fulfill this model, a potential merchant will have to find a supplier, purchase inventory and then store and ship product as orders come in. Because of this, you will have a fairly high startup cost and risk. On the other hand, This model gives you control over your product and fulfillment pipeline.

Manufacturer Model

The manufacturer model is similar to the merchant model, but involves you creating a product and either selling direct to the public or selling it to a retailer. volume and resources are primary restrictions to consider with this model. One of the big advantages of this model is that you essentially get to set your own price, so long as you cover cost of production. This can be either a large scale operation or a small scale one, and thus eatery cost differs. Fulfillment is handled much the same way as a merchant model or by a merchant you sell to.

Drop Shipping Model

The drop shipping model is closer to a classic web monetization focus while also maintaining elements of the traditional retail models. Drop shipping essentially means you take orders from customers and then contact your supplier who in turn fulfills the order for you. Drop shipping has a very low entry point, so a lot of people are crowding the market. One of the disadvantages of drop shipping is that you don't have control of your fulfillment process. It offers a bit more risk because of that but can have high returns on profit.

Affiliate Model

The affiliate model is a true blue web based model with no physical product and no fulfillment pipeline that you have to worry about. The affiliate model involves advertising for a company on your website and offering links to their products. When a customer buys one of their products through your link, you make a small commission. This isn't exactly a get rich quick model, it's a long game model if you want it to be profitable. I can see the affiliate model paring well with a following or a good content creator with a decent sum of fans.

My Choice

With all these models to choose from, I had to consider what my strengths were and what I was able to bring to the table. I don't have any money to seriously invest so merchant was out the door. I attempted drop shipping in the past but found that getting reliable suppliers is a very difficult task. I don't have the time to manufacture orders and don't want to spend money on maintaining a large website with a secure database, so manufacturing and eCommerce websites are out. This leaves me the affiliate model. I can build a simple front end website with links and host it on a CDN, this blog is proof of that. I figure that I could make a YouTube channel and hook my website up to it, start building an audience, and then apply for affiliate links.

So, in the end, because of the low eatery point, I've decided to build an affiliate website. Summer is right around the corner and people across the US are starting to fire up their grills, so maybe I can use my talent for BBQ and build a silly little blog/channel out of it to fulfill the requirements for the class. If I build a small audience along the way and make a penny or two after spending ten, I'd be ok with that.
